# Outreach operating system

Last updated: 2026-05-30

This is the repeatable workflow for turning an AI company into a concrete networking motion.

## Objective

Land conversations with high signal people in agentic AI, AI infrastructure, inference systems, developer tools, robotics AI, data infrastructure, evals, observability, model serving, and ML platforms.

The goal is not to ask for a job first. The goal is to show that you understand a real problem the team is working on, can reason technically about it, and can become useful.

## Pipeline

1. Source company people
   - Founders and technical executives.
   - Hiring managers and team leads.
   - Engineers writing company blogs, papers, docs, talks, or open source.
   - Recruiters only when tied to a relevant role or team.

2. Source open problems
   - Company blogs, docs, papers, changelogs, careers pages, talks, podcasts, GitHub, Hacker News, Reddit, and LinkedIn posts.
   - Look for repeated language: latency, cost, reliability, evals, observability, GPU utilization, orchestration, deployment, data quality, robotics generalization, human feedback, enterprise trust, safety, workflows.

3. Write the technical note
   - What problem are they solving?
   - Why is it hard right now?
   - What is the current technology landscape?
   - What open source projects, papers, or systems are relevant?
   - What small contribution could you make in 1 to 2 weeks?
   - What evidence would make the contribution credible?

4. Connect the problem to your adjacent proof
   - Similar systems you have built.
   - Coursework, projects, research, infra work, robotics/VLA work, backend systems, evals, deployment, data pipelines.
   - If you have not done the exact thing, explain the closest thing and how you would close the gap.

5. Pick the cold reach medium
   - Email from school email when possible.
   - LinkedIn for warmer professional context.
   - X only for people who are active there.
   - Conferences and in person networking for high trust follow up.

6. Draft outreach
   - 200 words max.
   - Write like a student.
   - Clear, human, specific.
   - No em dashes.
   - Avoid weird wording, hype, and overly polished phrasing.
   - Include one concrete technical observation and one direct ask.

7. Execute
   - Prioritize action over confusion.
   - Send 3 to 5 high quality notes per day.
   - Track every person, source, angle, message, sent date, reply, follow up date, and next step.

## Browser and approval workflow

Access surfaces to validate before the first send:

1. Gmail or school email
   - Verify compose access.
   - Do not draft, send, schedule, or attach anything without explicit approval.

2. Google Docs
   - Use for polished one-page technical notes.
   - Create a cloud document only after approval because it writes to the user's account.

3. Google Drive
   - Use for organizing one-pagers and tracking sheets.
   - Create folders/files only after approval.

4. LinkedIn
   - Use for profile verification, person sourcing, and messages when email is unavailable.
   - Do not connect, follow, react, comment, or message without explicit approval.

Approval gate before each send:

```text
Recipient:
Medium:
Subject:
Body:
Attachment or Google Doc link:
Send now or schedule:
If scheduled, exact date/time/timezone:
```

Operating rule: prepare everything first, then ask for approval at the last meaningful step before any external action.

## Outreach style

Good note shape:

```text
Hi NAME,

I am a student exploring work around SPECIFIC_PROBLEM. I read SOURCE and was interested in SPECIFIC_TECHNICAL_OBSERVATION.

I am trying to understand how teams like COMPANY think about RELATED_PROBLEM. I have been working on ADJACENT_PROOF, and I am writing a short technical note on POSSIBLE_CONTRIBUTION.

Would you be open to a quick 15 minute chat, or is there someone closer to this work you would recommend I speak with?

Best,
Jonathan
```

Bad note shape:

```text
I am passionate about AI and would love to join your team.
```

## Daily Hermes packet

Hermes should produce:

1. One company focus.
2. Three to five specific people.
3. One open problem with sources.
4. One technical note angle.
5. One adjacent proof angle.
6. One 200 word max outreach draft.
7. One action checklist for the day.

Hermes should not send generic market updates, generic job lists, or crypto content.
